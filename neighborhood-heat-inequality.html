As the temperatures increases, EMS calls for many heat-related illnesses rise

Hot temperatures are grouped into four categories: "not safe" for temps above 80 degrees, "caution" for temps ranging from 80 - 89 degrees, "extreme caution" for temps from 90 - 102 degrees and "danger" for temps over 103 degrees. This chart shows the rise and fall of emergency medical calls for certain health conditions in Baltimore in 2018 in each of these four categories. Each bar represents the number of hours between calls for a particular health condition in each category. For example, calls for dehydration and asthma all increase dramatically when temps raise from "not safe" to "danger." Select the button for each category to explore how hours between EMS calls changed at different temperatures.

High temps correlate with high poverty, lower life expectancy and more.

Temperatures across Baltimore can vary up to 10 degrees on a hot summer afternoon, according to a federally-funded study from 2018. This "urban heat island effect" is illustrated by neighborhood in the large map below. A CNS analysis of neighborhood demographics found a correlation between the temperature of a neighborhood and certain quality-of-life factors. Neighborhoods with a higher ambient temperature tend to have more families living below the poverty live, lower life expectancy, higher violent crime rates and a higher rate of employment then lower-temperature neighborhoods, as seen in the smaller maps below.

A heat wave hit Baltimore in July of 2019.

The seven-day stretch between July 16 and July 22 were the hottest consecutive days of the summer.

The heat index reached 111 degrees on July 21 at 8 p.m.

Capital News Service used indoor temperature sensors to record ambient temperature and humidity readings for the entire summer inside Stephanie Pingley’s home.

The sensor was placed in a second floor bedroom with no air conditioning.

The mean indoor heat index for that seven-day period was 98 degrees and the hourly average heat index climbed to 113.

The heat index in the bedroom never dropped below 89 degrees.

The heat index in Stephanie’s home consistently exceeded that of the outdoors.

On Friday, July 19 at 8 p.m., in the midst of the summer's most brutal heat wave, the heat index hit a blistering 106 degrees in the Inner Harbor.

Inside Stephanie Pingley's house, it was 7 degrees hotter, with a heat index of 113 degrees.

On Saturday, July 20, the heat index in Baltimore was still 94 degrees at 4 a.m. in the Inner Harbor.

Inside Stephanie Pingley's house, in the bedroom where two children sleep, it felt even hotter, with a heat index of 99 degrees.

Source: Federal Government weather data via Iowa State University and analyzed by CNS, CNS temperature and humidity sensor data

Heat-related deaths in the city of Baltimore and Maryland

There were 137 heat-related deaths recorded in Maryland between 2012 and 2018. Thirty seven of those deaths (nearly 30%) have occurred in Baltimore City. Maryland recorded 46 heat related deaths in 2012, when the United States was hit with one of the most severe heat waves in modern American history. There were 28 heat-related deaths in Maryland in 2018, 13 of which occurred in Baltimore. Many of those deaths were the result of a heat wave that hit Maryland in July of that year.

By Amina Lampkin
